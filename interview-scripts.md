# TalentOS Interview Scripts

**Product**: TalentOS -- AI-First Career Platform for Individual Job Seekers
**Stage**: Pre-MVP Discovery (No Users, No Code)
**Date**: 2026-07-10

These scripts supplement the quantitative experiments in `discovery-plan.md`. They provide the qualitative "why" behind the numbers.

---

# SCRIPT 1: Problem Discovery Interview

**Goal**: Validate the problem space -- is resume evaluation, matching, and skill understanding actually painful enough for job seekers?
**Duration**: 15-20 minutes
**Target Participants**: 5-8 active job seekers (applied to 3+ jobs in the past 30 days)
**Method**: Semi-structured interview, remote (video preferred, phone acceptable)

---

## Screen-Out Criteria (DO NOT interview these people)

Exclude anyone who:
- Has not applied to a job in the past 60 days (not actively searching)
- Works in recruiting/HR (their mental model is employer-side, not job-seeker-side)
- Has fewer than 3 years of professional experience (entry-level has fundamentally different needs)
- Currently has a recruiter or outplacement firm handling their search (outsourced the problem)
- Is a student or recent graduate (< 1 year out of school) -- separate study needed

## Pre-Interview Setup

- Send calendar invite with note: "We're researching how people approach career decisions. No prep needed."
- Record audio (with consent) but NOT video
- Have a notepad ready for non-verbal cues (hesitations, sighs, facial expressions)
- Prepare a simple Miro/Mural board to capture quotes in real-time

---

## Introduction (2 minutes)

> "Hi [name], thanks so much for making time today. I'm [your name], and I'm part of a team researching how people navigate their careers, especially during job transitions.
>
> There are no right or wrong answers here. I'm genuinely curious about YOUR experience. I may ask follow-up questions -- that's just me trying to understand better, not testing anything.
>
> This conversation will take about 15-20 minutes. I'd like to record the audio just so I don't miss anything -- is that okay? The recording will only be shared with our research team and won't be posted anywhere.
>
> Anything you share is confidential. If there's anything you'd prefer not to discuss, just say so. Sound good?"

[Pause. Wait for verbal confirmation before proceeding.]

**Facilitator note**: If they seem nervous, add: "Honestly, I'm just here to learn from you. Think of this as a casual conversation about your job search."

---

## Section A: Current Situation & Process (5-6 minutes)

### Q1. Tell me about your current job search situation. What prompted you to start looking?

[Probe if needed]:
- How long have you been searching?
- Are you looking to switch roles, switch companies, or both?
- Is this a "pull" (something better) or "push" (something wrong with current situation) move?

[**Listen for**: emotional state, urgency level, what triggered the search -- these shape pain tolerance]

### Q2. Walk me through what you did in the last week specifically for your job search. Step by step.

[Probe]:
- Where did you look for jobs? (job boards, referrals, LinkedIn, company sites)
- How many jobs did you apply to?
- How did you decide which ones to apply to?
- What took the most time?

[**Listen for**: time allocation, tools used, decision-making heuristics, frustration points]

### Q3. When you find a job posting that looks interesting, what do you do before you apply?

[Probe]:
- Do you tailor your resume? Every time, sometimes, or never?
- If you tailor it, what specifically do you change?
- How long does tailoring take you?
- Do you write a cover letter? Why or why not?

[**Listen for**: the gap between intention ("I should tailor") and reality ("I don't have time")]

### Q4. Think about the last resume you submitted. How confident were you that it was good?

[Probe]:
- What does "good" mean to you in this context?
- How did you evaluate it -- gut feel, feedback from someone, tool-based?
- Have you ever had your resume reviewed by someone else? Who?

[**Listen for**: self-evaluation confidence, existing evaluation methods, quality of feedback sources]

---

## Section B: Pain Points & Frustrations (4-5 minutes)

### Q5. What's the most frustrating part of the job search so far?

[Probe]:
- Why is that frustrating? (push for the root cause, not the symptom)
- Have you tried anything to solve that frustration? What happened?
- On a scale of 1-10, how much time/energy does this cost you per week?

[**Listen for**: emotional intensity, failed attempts to solve the problem, willingness to try new tools]

### Q6. Have you ever felt like you were applying to jobs blindly -- not knowing if you're a good fit or not?

[Probe]:
- What did that feel like?
- How did you decide whether to keep applying or give up on a particular role?
- Did you ever get feedback from a rejection that surprised you?

[**Listen for**: evidence of the "matching uncertainty" problem -- this is core to TalentOS value prop]

### Q7. Is there anything about your career history or skills that you find hard to articulate on a resume?

[Probe]:
- Career changers: How do you translate experience from one field to another?
- Gaps: How do you handle employment gaps or non-linear paths?
- Senior people: How do you condense 10+ years into 2 pages?

[**Listen for**: the "translation problem" -- AI could uniquely solve this]

---

## Section C: Existing Solutions & AI Experience (3-4 minutes)

### Q8. Have you ever used ChatGPT, Claude, or any AI tool for help with your job search? Tell me about that.

[Probe]:
- What did you ask it to do?
- What was the result like? Was it helpful?
- What was missing or disappointing about the output?
- Did you use it more than once, or just once?

[**Listen for**: the "good enough" threshold -- what does ChatGPT get right, where does it fall short]

### Q9. Do you use LinkedIn's resume features, job alerts, or any other career platform tools?

[Probe]:
- Which features specifically?
- What do you like about them?
- What do you wish they did differently?
- Have you paid for LinkedIn Premium or any career tool? What made you decide to pay (or not)?

[**Listen for**: existing willingness to pay, what they value vs. what they ignore]

### Q10. If a brand-new AI tool offered to analyze your resume and tell you exactly which jobs you're a strong match for -- and why -- what would be your honest first reaction?

[Probe]:
- What would make you trust it?
- What would make you NOT trust it?
- Would you upload your resume to an unknown platform? What conditions would need to be true?
- What would it need to show you to be worth coming back to?

[**Listen for**: trust threshold signals, privacy concerns, the "prove it to me" moment]

---

## Section D: Closing (2 minutes)

### Q11. If you could wave a magic wand and have ONE thing about your job search just work perfectly, what would it be?

[**Listen for**: the unfiltered, highest-priority pain -- this often reveals more than any structured question]

### Q12. Is there anything I should have asked but didn't? Anything about your experience you want to add?

### Q13. Would you be open to a follow-up conversation in a few weeks if we have something to show you?

[**Note their enthusiasm level, not just their "yes"**]

---

## Closing Script

> "Thank you so much, [name]. This has been incredibly helpful. Your experience is exactly what we need to hear as we figure out how to build something useful. I really appreciate your honesty.
>
> We'll be in touch if there's a next step. In the meantime, good luck with the search -- I hope you find something great."

---

## Facilitator Notes

**DO:**
- Take notes on emotional cues, not just words (sighs, pauses, tone changes, laughter)
- Let silences sit for 3-5 seconds -- people often fill silence with their most honest thoughts
- Ask "why" at least twice on any interesting answer -- the second "why" usually reveals the real insight
- Note specific language they use -- this becomes your copywriting gold later
- Pay attention to what they DON'T mention -- gaps in their story reveal unexamined assumptions

**DON'T:**
- Mention "TalentOS" or describe your product -- this is problem discovery, not a pitch
- Ask leading questions like "Wouldn't it be nice if..." -- let them define what "magical" looks like
- Argue or defend any position if they dismiss AI tools -- just understand why
- Ask more than one question at a time -- it dilutes the answer
- Rush through the closing questions -- Q11 ("magic wand") often produces the best insight

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Participant describes a problem but shows no emotional investment (nice-to-have, not must-have)
- "I've never thought about that" to core TalentOS assumptions (problem may not exist for them)
- Extreme reluctance to discuss resume upload (trust problem may be insurmountable for this segment)
- Answers feel rehearsed or performative (they're telling you what they think you want to hear)

**Post-interview immediate notes** (fill out within 30 minutes while fresh):
- Top 3 most surprising things they said
- Quote that best captures their pain
- Anything that contradicted your assumptions
- Emotional temperature: how frustrated vs. resigned vs. optimistic?

# SCRIPT 2: Concept Validation Interview

**Goal**: Test the value proposition by showing mockups/concepts and gauging reaction, trust, and willingness to use/pay.
**Duration**: 20-25 minutes
**Target Participants**: 8-12 active job seekers who have completed Script 1 or equivalent problem-discovery conversation
**Method**: Semi-structured interview with concept mockups (Figma, static images, or printed materials)

---

## Screen-Out Criteria (DO NOT interview these people)

Exclude anyone who:
- Did NOT participate in Script 1 or a prior problem-discovery conversation (they lack context for comparison)
- Self-selected as "not interested in AI career tools" during Script 1 screening (their mind is made up -- low signal value)
- Are currently employed and not actively searching (concepts feel hypothetical, not visceral)
- Work in UX/design or product management (they'll evaluate the mockup quality, not the concept)

## Pre-Interview Setup

- Prepare 4 concept exhibits (Figma frames or printed sheets, one per concept below)
- Have a real-ish job posting ready (not a made-up one -- use a real posting from a well-known company)
- Prepare 3 versions of career advice at different specificity levels (see Section C below)
- Screen-share setup tested (or physical materials printed and numbered)
- Timer visible -- you WILL run over if you don't manage time

## Concept Exhibits to Prepare

**Exhibit A -- AI Resume X-Ray**: A structured analysis showing:
- Overall fit score (e.g., 72/100) with a visual gauge
- Category breakdown (Experience: 85, Skills: 60, Education: 90, Keywords: 55)
- Top 3 strengths (e.g., "Your 5 years of product management align with the core requirement")
- Gap table: What's missing + how critical each gap is (Critical / Important / Nice-to-have)
- 2-3 specific rewrite suggestions with before/after text
- "Talking points for your interview" -- 3 things to emphasize

**Exhibit B -- Match X-Ray**: A job match card showing:
- Job title + company name + salary range
- Match score (e.g., "83% Fit")
- 3-4 bullet explanations: "Your React experience covers their frontend needs. You're missing AWS (required in 80% of similar roles). Your leadership of a 5-person team exceeds their 3-person requirement."
- One-click: "Tailor my resume for this job"

**Exhibit C -- Before/After Resume Diff**: A side-by-side or track-changes view showing:
- Original resume text on the left
- AI-suggested rewrite on the right
- Inline annotations explaining WHY each change was made (linked to specific JD requirements)
- Confidence indicator per change ("high confidence" / "suggestion, review recommended")

**Exhibit D -- Specificity Ladder** (3 levels of advice for the same situation):
- Level 1 (Generic): "Consider developing your leadership skills. Team management is valued in senior roles."
- Level 2 (Specific to user): "Your experience leading a cross-functional sprint team maps well to this role's 'team leadership' requirement. Highlighting the 4-person team you led at [Company] would strengthen this section."
- Level 3 (Hyper-specific): "Apply to Stripe's PM role (req #4821). Reach out to their hiring manager Jane Chen on LinkedIn. Your Stripe-like payment experience is exactly what they need."

---

## Introduction (2-3 minutes)

> "Hi [name], thanks for coming back. Last time we talked about your job search experience and challenges. Today is a bit different -- I'm going to show you some concepts we're exploring and get your honest reaction.
>
> A few ground rules:
> - These are CONCEPTS, not finished products. They may look rough -- that's intentional.
> - There are no right answers. I want to know what resonates, what doesn't, and why.
> - I may push back on your reactions -- not to argue, but to understand your thinking more deeply.
> - Your candid criticism is MORE valuable than polite praise. We need to know what's broken.
>
> Ready?"

[Pause for confirmation.]

**Facilitator note**: Emphasize "no right answers" and "criticism is more valuable" -- participants tend to be overly positive about concepts they see in an interview context. Prime them for honesty.

---

## Section A: Resume X-Ray Concept (6-7 minutes)

### Q1. I'm going to show you a concept called the "AI Resume X-Ray." Take a moment to look at it, then tell me what you see.

[Let them look for 30-60 seconds without prompting. Take notes on where their eyes go first, what they read, what they skip.]

[Probe]:
- What's your gut reaction?
- What do you notice first?
- What's confusing or unclear?

### Q2. Imagine you uploaded your resume and a job description, and this is what came back. Walk me through what you would do with this information.

[Probe]:
- Would you trust this analysis? Why or why not?
- What would make you trust it MORE?
- What would make you trust it LESS?
- Which part would you act on first?
- Is anything missing that you'd want to see?

[**Listen for**: behavioral intent -- not "do you like it" but "would you actually DO something differently"]

### Q3. That fit score of 72 out of 100 -- how would you react to seeing that number?

[Probe]:
- What does that number mean to you?
- Would you apply to that job? Why or why not?
- Would you feel discouraged or motivated by 72?
- What number would you need to see to feel confident about applying?

[**Listen for**: score sensitivity -- this is a make-or-break design decision. Too high = no motivation. Too low = demoralization. We need the emotional response, not just the rational one.]

### Q4. The rewrite suggestions -- changing specific lines in your resume -- how do you feel about AI editing your resume?

[Probe]:
- Would you accept the suggestions as-is, or always modify them?
- What would make you confident the AI "got it right"?
- What would make you think it made it WORSE?
- How important is it that you can see WHY a change was suggested?

[**Listen for**: control vs. convenience tension, and whether explainability ("why this change") actually matters to them]

---

## Section B: Match X-Ray Concept (4-5 minutes)

### Q5. Here's another concept -- the "Match X-Ray." This shows you a specific job and explains why it matches your profile. What's your reaction?

[Let them absorb. Note what they read first, what they linger on.]

[Probe]:
- If you saw this for a job you hadn't considered, would it change your behavior?
- The "why" explanations -- do they add value, or are they just noise?
- What questions do you have that this DOESN'T answer?

### Q6. Compare this to what LinkedIn does today (showing you "matched" jobs). What's different about this?

[Probe]:
- Is this meaningfully better, or just different?
- What would LinkedIn need to add for you to not need this?
- What can LinkedIn NEVER do that this could?

[**Listen for**: the competitive moat -- is there a genuine gap, or is this incremental?]

### Q7. Would you want this for every job posting you see, or only for specific ones?

[Probe]:
- All jobs, top matches only, or jobs you're uncertain about?
- Would you use this to EXPLORE new career directions, or only to evaluate roles you've already found?

[**Listen for**: discovery use case vs. evaluation use case -- this shapes the product scope]

---

## Section C: Specificity Preference Test (5-6 minutes)

### Q8. Now I'm going to show you three different levels of career advice for the same scenario. Read all three, then I'll ask you some questions.

[Show Level 1, Level 2, Level 3 in RANDOMIZED ORDER (counterbalance across participants). Use physical cards if in person, or labeled sections on screen.]

[Let them read all three without interruption.]

### Q9. Which of these three feels most useful to you? Most actionable?

[Probe]:
- Why that one?
- What makes the others less useful?
- On a 1-5 scale, how trustworthy does each one feel? (Have them rate all three)

### Q10. Level 3 mentions a specific company and a specific person. Does that feel helpful or does it feel... strange?

[Probe]:
- Would you actually reach out to that person?
- Does that make you trust the system more or less?
- At what point does "specific" cross the line into "creepy" or "unreliable"?
- If the system couldn't guarantee that specific company/person was a real match, would it be worse to include it or not?

[**Listen for**: the specificity-credibility tradeoff. This is assumption A3 from the discovery plan -- the sweet spot between helpful and hallucination.]

### Q11. If you had to choose: would you prefer an AI that's RIGHT about 70% of the time and tells you specific things, or one that's RIGHT 95% of the time but speaks in generalities?

[Probe]:
- What if it was RIGHT 70% of the time but told you WHEN it was uncertain?
- Does knowing the confidence level change your answer?

[**Listen for**: transparency as a trust bridge. This tests assumption A4.]

---

## Section D: Before/After Diff Concept (3 minutes)

### Q12. This concept shows a before-and-after view of your resume with tracked changes, like Google Docs suggestions. What's your immediate reaction?

[Probe]:
- Would you use this feature? In what situation?
- Do you want to accept/reject changes individually, or apply all at once?
- The annotations explain why each change was made -- does that matter to you?
- How does this compare to just asking ChatGPT to rewrite your resume?

[**Listen for**: whether the structured diff provides enough incremental value over free-form ChatGPT rewrites]

---

## Section E: Trust, Willingness to Use, Willingness to Pay (3-4 minutes)

### Q13. After seeing all of these concepts, what would your FIRST interaction with this platform look like? What would you do first?

[Probe]:
- Would you upload your resume immediately, or explore the platform first?
- What would you need to see to feel comfortable uploading your resume?
- What would make you leave immediately?

[**Listen for**: onboarding friction signals and trust barriers]

### Q14. If this tool existed today and worked as shown, how often would you use it?

[Probe]:
- Once? Weekly? Only when applying to a specific job?
- What would bring you back after the first use?
- What would make you tell a friend about it?

[**Listen for**: natural usage frequency, word-of-mouth triggers]

### Q15. Would you pay for this? What pricing would feel fair?

[Probe]:
- Monthly subscription vs. per-use vs. freemium?
- What would the free tier need to include for you to try it?
- What would the paid tier need to include for you to upgrade?
- Name a number -- what's the most you'd pay per month?
- Name a number -- what's the LEAST useful it could be and you'd still try it free?

[**Listen for**: pricing anchors, the gap between stated and actual willingness to pay]

---

## Section F: Closing (2 minutes)

### Q16. Of everything you've seen today, what's the single most compelling feature? The one thing that would actually make you switch from your current approach?

### Q17. What's the single biggest concern or reason you might NOT use this?

### Q18. Any other thoughts, ideas, or concerns you want to share before we wrap up?

### Q19. Would you be interested in testing a working prototype in the next few weeks?

---

## Closing Script

> "This has been incredibly valuable, [name]. Your feedback directly shapes what we build and how we build it. I especially appreciate your honesty about [reference something specific they said that was candid].
>
> We'll be in touch about prototype testing. Good luck with the job search -- and honestly, based on what you told me about your background, you've got strong experience. Keep going.
>
> Thanks again."

---

## Facilitator Notes

**DO:**
- RANDOMIZE the order you show the 3 specificity levels -- counterbalance across participants (if 12 participants, 4 see L1-L2-L3, 4 see L2-L3-L1, 4 see L3-L1-L2)
- Note the FIRST thing they look at on each exhibit -- eye tracking tells you what grabs attention vs. what they skip
- Use "Think Aloud" protocol: "As you look at this, can you tell me what you're thinking?"
- Ask them to RATE things on scales when possible -- "1-5" is easier to compare across participants than open-ended reactions
- Capture their EXACT WORDS for key reactions -- these become copywriting and messaging input

**DON'T:**
- Show all concepts at once -- reveal them one at a time to avoid overwhelm
- Defend the concepts if they criticize them -- just ask "why?" and "what would be better?"
- Let them spend more than 90 seconds looking at a concept in silence -- redirect: "What are you thinking?"
- Ask "Do you like it?" (everyone says yes) -- instead ask "Would you use it?" and "What would you change?"
- Let the pricing question feel like a negotiation -- frame it as "help us understand what's fair"

**Critical thing to measure**:
- After showing ALL concepts together at the end, ask them to pick ONE that is the "killer feature" -- the one they'd tell a friend about. This reveals the core value prop from the user's perspective, not ours.

**Post-interview immediate notes**:
- Which concept got the strongest emotional reaction? (positive OR negative)
- Did the specificity ladder reveal a clear preference?
- Any participant say they would NOT upload their resume? Why?
- Did trust concerns dominate, or did excitement about features dominate?
- Was there a clear "aha moment" during the interview?

# SCRIPT 3: Retention & Engagement Interview

**Goal**: Validate retention mechanisms -- what would bring users back after the initial resume analysis?
**Duration**: 15-20 minutes
**Target Participants**: 5-8 users who completed Script 2 and reacted positively to at least one concept
**Method**: Semi-structured interview with concept materials for the "Career Pulse" and social features

---

## Screen-Out Criteria (DO NOT interview these people)

Exclude anyone who:
- Did not complete Script 2 or equivalent concept testing
- Expressed zero interest in any TalentOS concept during Script 2 (no anchor to build from)
- Currently uses NO digital tools in their job search (they live in a different paradigm)
- Is a passive job seeker only exploring casually (retention matters less for passive seekers)

## Pre-Interview Setup

- Prepare a mock "Career Pulse" email example (realistic, personalized to their stated career interests from Script 2)
- Prepare a mock "peer signal" example ("X people like you applied to similar roles this week")
- Have 3-4 alternative retention concept descriptions ready (see Section B below)
- Have a simple frequency/preference matrix printed or on-screen for them to fill in

## Career Pulse Mockup to Prepare

A realistic email mockup containing:
- Subject line: "Your Career Pulse: 3 new matches + market signal"
- 2-3 job match cards (title, company, fit score, one-line "why")
- 1 market signal: "Demand for [specific skill] grew 15% in your target market this month"
- 1 skill gap update: "Since your last check: 2 new roles match your updated profile"
- CTA: "View your full Career Snapshot"

---

## Introduction (2 minutes)

> "Hi [name], great to see you again. This is our third conversation, and you've been incredibly generous with your time and feedback.
>
> Today I want to focus on something specific: after you get that initial resume analysis and job matches, what happens next? What brings you back?
>
> Again, no right answers. I want to understand your natural behavior, not what you think I want to hear. If something doesn't interest you, tell me -- that's just as valuable as enthusiasm.
>
> Ready?"

---

## Section A: Post-Analysis Behavior (3-4 minutes)

### Q1. Let's imagine you've used TalentOS, got your Resume X-Ray, reviewed your matches, and tailored your resume. It's been 3 days. What happens next in your job search?

[Probe]:
- Do you come back to the platform? Under what circumstances?
- What would you be looking for that's different from your first visit?
- Or do you move on to other tools/platfoms? Which ones?

[**Listen for**: the natural "what's next" moment -- this is where retention has to earn its place]

### Q2. Think about a tool or app you use regularly (doesn't have to be career-related). What makes you go back to it?

[Probe]:
- Is it new content? Progress tracking? Notifications? Social features?
- How do they decide HOW OFTEN to reach out to you?
- What would make you UNSUBSCRIBE or delete an app?

[**Listen for**: their personal retention model -- what hooks work for THEM specifically. This is generalizable.]

### Q3. If you were job searching and received a personalized email every Monday morning with new job matches based on your skills and preferences -- how would you feel about that?

[Probe]:
- Would you open it? Every week? For how long?
- What content in the email would make you click through?
- What would make you stop opening it?
- Too frequent? Too infrequent? What's the right rhythm?

[**Listen for**: email fatigue threshold and content preferences]

---

## Section B: Career Pulse Concept (5-6 minutes)

### Q4. I'm going to show you a mockup of something we're calling the "Career Pulse." It's a personalized weekly email with job matches, market signals, and updates about your career profile. Take a look.

[Show the mockup. Let them read it.]

### Q5. What's your immediate reaction? Be honest -- would you open this email if it landed in your inbox?

[Probe]:
- What caught your eye? What did you skip?
- Is this useful, interesting, or just more noise?
- How does this compare to job alerts you already get from LinkedIn/Indeed/other platforms?

### Q6. We're thinking about including "market signals" -- things like "demand for [skill] grew 15% this month" or "your target roles have 20% more openings this week." Does that information interest you?

[Probe]:
- Why or why not?
- Would that actually change your behavior (e.g., upskill, adjust targets)?
- Or is it just interesting trivia?
- What OTHER market signals would be genuinely useful?

[**Listen for**: whether market intelligence is a retention hook or just wallpaper. This tests assumption A9 indirectly.]

### Q7. What would make this email so valuable that you'd be annoyed if you STOPPED receiving it?

[Probe]:
- Is there a single piece of content that would create that kind of attachment?
- Or does it need to be consistently good across all sections?
- Think about the last email newsletter you actually cared about -- what made it different?

[**Listen for**: the "can't live without it" threshold -- this is the bar for true retention]

---

## Section C: Return Triggers & Frequency (4-5 minutes)

### Q8. Let's think about different scenarios that might bring you back to the platform between weekly emails. Which of these would actually make you log in?

[Read each, one at a time. Rate 1-5 or thumbs up/down]:
- "A new job posting that's a 90%+ match for your profile"
- "Your resume score changed because you updated it"
- "Someone in your industry got hired at a company you're targeting"
- "A skill you have is trending up/down in demand"
- "Your Career Pulse shows new jobs since last week"
- "A new resume tip relevant to your target role"
- "A comparison: how your profile stacks up against similar candidates (anonymized)"

[Probe on any that get strong reactions (positive or negative):
- Why does that one resonate?
- Would you act on it immediately, or just note it?
- What would the action look like?]

### Q9. During an active job search, how long does a typical search last for you?

[Probe]:
- 2 weeks? 3 months? 6 months?
- At what point does searching start to feel like a grind?
- Is there a natural "I'm done" signal, or does it trail off?

[**Listen for**: search duration and fatigue curve -- this tells us the engagement window]

### Q10. If you got a job offer tomorrow, would you want to keep receiving Career Pulse emails?

[Probe]:
- What would you want to happen? (pause, unsubscribe, switch to "career growth" mode?)
- Is there a version of this product that's useful EVEN WHEN you're not searching?

[**Listen for**: the post-hire lifecycle -- could TalentOS have a life beyond job search?]

---

## Section D: Social & Community Interest (3 minutes)

### Q11. Would you be interested in seeing anonymized information about other job seekers like you -- for example, "7 people with a similar background applied to roles like this this week"?

[Probe]:
- Does that feel motivating ("I'm not alone") or demoralizing ("too much competition")?
- Would you want to know MORE or LESS about what other candidates are doing?
- What's the line between helpful peer data and uncomfortable surveillance?

[**Listen for**: social proof vs. competitive anxiety tension. This tests assumption A5-2.]

### Q12. Would you ever want to connect with other job seekers on this platform? For example, anonymous groups for people targeting the same role or industry.

[Probe]:
- Under what conditions? (anonymity level, group size, format)
- Is this a "nice to have" or would it actually help your search?
- Where do you currently go for career community/support? (Reddit, Discord, Slack groups, LinkedIn?)

[**Listen for**: community as retention or community as distraction. This is a v2 feature -- we need to know if it's worth building.]

### Q13. Is there anything about your job search that feels lonely or isolating that a platform could address?

[Probe]:
- What do you wish you could talk to someone about?
- Who do you currently talk to about your job search?
- Would an AI advisor (chat-based) fill that gap, or would you need a human?

[**Listen for**: emotional support needs -- this could be a powerful retention hook OR a liability]

---

## Section E: Closing (2 minutes)

### Q14. If you could design the perfect "second visit" experience for a career platform, what would it show you?

[**Listen for**: their ideal retention moment -- this is the product spec they're writing for you]

### Q15. What's the ONE thing that would make you recommend this platform to a friend who's job searching?

### Q16. Any final thoughts on what we've discussed today?

### Q17. Would you be willing to beta test a working version of this product?

## Closing Script

## Facilitator Notes

**DO:**
- Ground all questions in their SPECIFIC situation from previous interviews -- reference their career goals, their stated pain points, their reactions from Script 2
- Ask them to compare Career Pulse to alerts they already get (LinkedIn, Indeed) -- the delta matters
- Track which "return triggers" (Q8) generate the strongest emotional response -- these become prioritized features
- Note their search duration estimate (Q9) -- this is critical for product metrics (retention window)
- Ask about their CURRENT community/support sources -- this reveals whether community is a TalentOS problem to solve or not

**DON'T:**
- Assume retention features are wanted -- many job seekers want to LEAVE the platform (they found a job!)
- Push social features if they show no interest -- community is expensive to build and may not be needed
- Ask about "engagement" in product terms -- use their language: "come back," "check it," "open the email"
- Over-invest in the Career Pulse concept if they clearly prefer on-demand vs. push notifications

**Key insight to extract**:
The core tension in retention for a job search product is this: the better it works, the faster they leave. A successful retention strategy either (a) serves users beyond the search (career growth mode), or (b) provides ongoing value during an extended search. Push hard to understand which of these (or both) resonates.

**Post-interview immediate notes**:
- Did Career Pulse get a "yes, I want this" or a "maybe, if..."?
- Which return triggers scored highest?
- Social features: enthusiastic, indifferent, or uncomfortable?
- Did anyone mention a "post-job-search" use case unprompted?
- What was the single strongest retention signal across all participants?
- Did anyone say they would NOT want weekly emails? Why?
